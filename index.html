Coverage Types

What Gets Covered

A complete commercial protection stack for mobile home park owners — from common area liability to property damage, lost rent, and the umbrella above it all.

⚖️

Commercial General Liability

Your most critical exposure. Covers bodily injury and property damage claims arising from the park's common areas and premises — the roads, walkways, laundry, rec rooms, and more.

  • Slip-and-fall on park roads, walkways, or common areas
  • Tenant or guest injury in the clubhouse or pool area
  • Property damage to a third party arising from park operations
  • Defense costs even for groundless claims
  • Personal injury and advertising liability
🏢

Commercial Property

Covers the structures and infrastructure that you own as the park operator — not the individual tenant homes, but everything the park itself consists of.

  • Clubhouse, community center, and recreation facilities
  • Laundry building and maintenance office
  • Park-owned roads, signage, and fencing
  • Utility systems (water, sewer, electrical pedestals)
  • Park-owned manufactured homes (if applicable)
💵

Loss of Rental Income

When a covered loss forces units offline and you can't collect lot rent or unit rent, business income coverage replaces the lost revenue stream during the repair period.

  • Lost lot rent when lots are rendered uninhabitable
  • Lost unit rent if you own the homes
  • Extended indemnity for longer rebuilds
  • Extra expense to speed up repairs
👷

Workers' Compensation

Required in most states if you employ maintenance workers, office staff, or groundskeepers. MHP maintenance involves real physical risk — injuries happen.

  • Medical expenses for injured employees
  • Lost wages during recovery
  • Employer's liability protection
  • Covers part-time and seasonal staff in most states
🛡️

Commercial Umbrella

Mobile home parks face elevated premises liability risk. An umbrella policy sits above your GL and other underlying policies, adding $1M–$10M+ in additional protection.

  • Catastrophic injury claims beyond GL limits
  • Multi-party lawsuits (tenant + guests + visitors)
  • Stack over GL, auto, and employer's liability
  • Critical for parks with pools, playgrounds, or high foot traffic
🌊

Flood Insurance

Standard commercial property policies exclude flood. Many MHPs sit on low-lying land near rivers, drainage basins, or in FEMA flood zones — a separate flood policy is essential.

  • Infrastructure damage from surface flooding
  • Road washout and utility system damage
  • Available through NFIP and private flood markets
  • Covers park-owned structures (not individual tenant homes)

💡 Example Claim

The Cracked Sidewalk Lawsuit

A tenant in a 90-lot manufactured housing community trips on a cracked and heaved concrete walkway in the park's common area. She falls, breaks her wrist, and is unable to work for six weeks. She retains an attorney and files suit against the park owner for medical expenses, lost wages, and pain and suffering — totaling $120,000.

The park owner's Commercial General Liability policy defends the claim and negotiates a settlement. The park's commercial property policy also covers the cost of replacing the deteriorated walkway sections under the "business premises" property coverage — preventing the same hazard from injuring another resident.

Without GL coverage: The park owner pays defense costs and the settlement out of pocket — potentially $150,000 or more by the time it's resolved.
With proper MHP insurance: The insurer takes over defense, negotiates the settlement, and the park owner pays only the deductible. The park stays operational.

Frequently Asked Questions

Mobile Home Park Insurance
Questions — Answered

The questions we hear most from MHP owners and manufactured housing community operators, answered directly.

Mobile home park insurance is commercial insurance for the park owner — it covers the park's common areas, infrastructure, roads, utility systems, clubhouses, laundry facilities, and office buildings. It also provides General Liability for tenant injuries in common areas. Homeowner's insurance (or a tenant's HO-7 manufactured home policy) is what each individual tenant needs to cover their own unit and personal belongings. As the park owner, you don't insure the homes your tenants own — your tenants do. Your policy protects the park itself.
Yes — and you should require it as a lease condition. Each tenant is responsible for insuring their own manufactured home or mobile home with an HO-7 policy. Your commercial park insurance does not cover individual tenant homes, their contents, or liability arising from within their private lot. Many park owners require proof of renter's or homeowner's insurance at lease signing. This protects both parties: tenants are covered for their home, and you're not fielding calls about damage that is their responsibility.
If you own homes within the park and rent them out as units (called park-owned homes, or POHs), those homes must be listed on your commercial property policy — they're treated like any landlord-owned rental dwelling, and your policy covers the structure. Tenant-owned homes sitting on leased lots are the tenant's responsibility to insure under their own policy. Many parks have a mix of both, and getting this distinction right on your policy is critical. Misclassifying POHs as tenant-owned can leave you uninsured for a structure you own if it burns or is destroyed by a storm.
Standard commercial property policies exclude flooding — this applies to mobile home parks just as it does to any commercial real estate. If your park is in a FEMA flood zone (Zones A, AE, or the 500-year zone) or near a river, creek, or low-lying drainage area, you need a separate commercial flood policy through the NFIP or private flood markets. Flooding is one of the most serious underinsured risks for MHP owners — especially for park infrastructure like roads, utility connections, and the areas around and under mobile homes. We'll check your park's flood zone status when we quote you.
If a tenant damages park-owned infrastructure — underground utility lines, water or sewer connections, electrical pedestals, or common-area property — your commercial property policy may cover the repair depending on the cause of loss and your policy terms. If the tenant's negligence caused the damage, you can typically pursue recovery against the tenant and their liability policy. This is one more reason why requiring tenants to carry their own renters insurance (with liability coverage) protects you as the park owner. We can help you structure both policies to work together correctly.
Yes. Loss of rental income — also called business income coverage — pays you when a covered property loss forces lots or units offline and you lose lot rent or unit rent during the repair period. For a park with 50–200 lots, even a partial event that takes 20 lots offline for 90 days represents tens of thousands of dollars in lost income. This coverage is especially important for parks with park-owned homes, where both the structure and the rent stream are at risk from the same event. We'll make sure the coverage limit reflects your actual annual rent roll so you're not underinsured.
Absolutely — and for MHP owners, umbrella coverage is highly recommended. Mobile home parks face above-average premises liability exposure because of aging infrastructure, common areas used by many residents, and high foot traffic from guests and family members. A commercial umbrella sits above your General Liability and any other scheduled underlying policies, extending your protection to $1M–$10M+ above the base GL limits. Given the litigation environment around serious tenant injury claims, umbrella is one of the most cost-effective additions to an MHP insurance program. We routinely include umbrella options in our MHP quotes.
Yes — if you have employees (maintenance workers, office staff, groundskeepers, or even part-time seasonal help), most states legally require Workers' Compensation coverage. MHP maintenance work involves real physical risk: electrical repairs, plumbing work under homes, operating machinery, and outdoor work in variable conditions. A maintenance employee injury without Workers' Comp can expose you to significant personal liability and state penalties in addition to the medical costs. Even if you classify workers as part-time, they likely trigger coverage requirements in most states. We'll check your state's specific rules and include Workers' Comp in your quote if needed.
